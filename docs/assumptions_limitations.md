# Assumptions and Limitations

This document outlines the key assumptions underlying the storm-frequency–driven flood exposure framework and the limitations that should be considered when interpreting results.

These assumptions are stated explicitly to support transparent, responsible, and ethical use of the analysis.

---

## 1. Conceptual Assumptions

### 1.1 Non-Stationarity of Extreme Rainfall
The framework assumes that extreme rainfall frequency is **non-stationary** under current and future climate conditions.

Rather than treating historical return periods as fixed, the analysis evaluates how the **frequency of threshold-exceeding events changes over time**, both in observed records and climate model projections.

This assumption reflects current climate science consensus and motivates the use of epoch-based comparisons rather than stationary probability models.

---

### 1.2 Frequency as a Driver of Flood Risk
The analysis assumes that **increasing storm frequency contributes to increased flood risk**, even in the absence of changes in storm magnitude.

This includes:
- reduced recovery time between events,
- increased likelihood of soil saturation,
- higher probability of compound or cascading impacts.

The framework focuses on **risk amplification through repetition**, not prediction of individual flood events.

---

## 2. Data and Methodological Assumptions

### 2.1 Threshold-Based Definition of Extreme Rainfall
Extreme rainfall events are defined using a **fixed precipitation threshold** (e.g., ≥ 3 inches in 24 hours), held constant across time and datasets.

This assumes:
- threshold exceedance is a meaningful proxy for flood-relevant precipitation,
- consistency across time is preferable to dynamically shifting thresholds for detecting change.

Threshold selection influences absolute event counts but not the direction of frequency change.

---

### 2.2 Use of FEMA Flood Hazard Zones as Context
FEMA Flood Insurance Rate Map (FIRM) polygons are used as **hazard context**, not as deterministic inundation predictions.

Flood zones represent areas of historically elevated flood probability, not guarantees of flooding during any specific storm.

The analysis assumes flood hazard zones are appropriate for identifying **relative exposure patterns**, not event-specific impacts.

---

### 2.3 Areal-Weighted Population Distribution
Population exposure is estimated using **areal-weighted spatial intersection** between census tracts and flood hazard polygons.

This assumes:
- population is approximately proportional to area within tracts,
- finer-scale population distribution data (e.g., parcel-level occupancy) is unavailable or inconsistent across regions.

Areal weighting is a conservative approximation and may under- or over-estimate exposure in tracts with highly clustered populations.

---

### 2.4 Census Data as Proxy for Vulnerability
Census and ACS variables are used as proxies for social vulnerability.

This assumes:
- demographic and socioeconomic indicators reflect structural vulnerability,
- census tracts are an appropriate spatial unit for population analysis.

The framework does not attempt to model individual-level vulnerability or adaptive capacity.

---

## 3. Aggregation and Scale Limitations

### 3.1 County-Level Aggregation
Exposure metrics are aggregated to the **county scale** for reporting and interpretation.

This choice reflects:
- relevance to emergency management and hazard mitigation planning,
- alignment with funding and policy frameworks.

County-level aggregation masks intra-county variation and should not be interpreted as uniform risk within counties.

---

### 3.2 Spatial Resolution Constraints
Results are constrained by:
- the spatial resolution of PRISM and CORDEX data,
- the scale of census tract geometries,
- the generalized nature of flood hazard polygons.

The analysis is not intended for parcel-level, site-specific, or engineering design decisions.

---

## 4. Climate Model Limitations (CORDEX)

### 4.1 Model Uncertainty
Future frequency estimates are derived from regional climate model simulations and are subject to:
- structural model uncertainty,
- emissions scenario assumptions,
- internal climate variability.

Results are interpreted as **directional signals**, not deterministic forecasts.

---

### 4.2 Model Selection
A limited set of CORDEX models is used to demonstrate methodological applicability.

The framework assumes that **consistency across multiple models** strengthens confidence in observed patterns but does not replace full ensemble analysis.

---

## 5. Interpretation Boundaries

### 5.1 Not an Inundation Model
This framework does **not** simulate flood depths, velocities, or extents for specific events.

Hydraulic and hydrologic modeling would be required for event-specific inundation analysis.

---

### 5.2 Not a Risk Prediction Tool
The analysis does not predict:
- the timing of future floods,
- specific damage outcomes,
- individual household impacts.

It is intended to identify **where risk pressure is increasing**, not to forecast discrete disasters.

---

## 6. Ethical and Responsible Use

Results should be used to:
- inform preparedness and mitigation planning,
- prioritize resources equitably,
- identify communities facing cumulative hazard exposure.

Results should **not** be used to:
- deny insurance, aid, or assistance,
- stigmatize communities,
- replace local knowledge or lived experience.

---

## 7. Summary

This framework emphasizes transparency, replicability, and interpretability over false precision.

Its value lies in identifying **systemic risk trends under climate non-stationarity**, not in providing definitive predictions.

Users are encouraged to pair these results with local expertise, additional data sources, and context-specific analyses.
