# Storm Frequency–Driven Flood Risk: Conceptual Overview

## Research Motivation
Flood risk is often communicated using static categories (e.g., “100-year flood”), even as climate change alters the frequency of extreme rainfall events that generate flooding.

This project reframes flood risk around **changing storm frequency** and asks:

> How does an increase in extreme rainfall events translate into more frequent flooding, and who is most exposed when it does?

---

## Conceptual Framework
The analysis links four components:

1. **Historical Storm Frequency**
   - Observed changes in extreme rainfall events using PRISM data

2. **Future Storm Frequency**
   - Projected changes using CORDEX regional climate model output

3. **Flood Hazard Context**
   - River and floodplain information used to contextualize how frequency shifts affect flood probability

4. **Population Vulnerability**
   - Census-based demographic indicators used to identify who is most exposed to repeated flood risk

Rather than treating these as separate analyses, the project integrates them into a single, replicable framework.

---

## Key Insight
Flood risk increases not only when storms become more intense, but when they become **more frequent**—reducing recovery time, increasing cumulative exposure, and disproportionately affecting vulnerable populations.

This project emphasizes **non-stationarity**, cumulative risk, and decision relevance over traditional static hazard metrics.

---

## Intended Use
The framework is designed for:
- Emergency management
- Hazard mitigation planning
- Environmental justice analysis
- Replication in other regions

It prioritizes transparency, modularity, and interpretability over black-box modeling.

---

## Limitations
- Flood hazard layers are used as contextual indicators, not event-specific inundation models.
- Results do not replace local hydraulic studies or community knowledge.
- Projections reflect model-based frequency change, not certainty.

These limitations are documented explicitly to support responsible use.
